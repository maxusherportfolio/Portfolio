Arduino powered collective

Background

For my Dad's birthday I wanted to get him a collecitve that he could used in his helicopter sumulator games. The ones that were available on the market started at almost $400 for a very simple one with one or two buttons and the very cheap ones started at $100 for just a handle you could pivot, so I decided to build him one myself that would give the most functionality for a very reasonable price.

Process

The design process began with setting a clear set of goals, the primary objective being the incorporation of multiple functional inputs. I sketched several concepts, each with distinct features, and selected the most practical elements from each to integrate into the final design. With these components in mind, I started building a rough model in SolidWorks. This initial model helped me better understand the overall scale and identify where key hardware elements would be placed. From there, I iterated on the design to ensure I used the most cost-effective parts available, such as a PVC pipe for the main stalk and a potentiometer for angle input.

Given that the usual 3D printing resources were unavailable during the semester, I needed to pay per gram for printing from an outside source. This made optimizing the 3D-printed components critical, as I had to strike a balance between minimizing material usage and ensuring sufficient strength. Using SolidWorks, I performed FEA to verify that each part could withstand the expected stresses and impacts. Once I was confident in the design's durability, I printed the necessary parts and began assembly.

For the input system, I selected three buttons, two toggle switches, and a three-way momentary toggle, all mounted on the head of the stalk. I carefully routed the wiring down the stalk to the base, where the Arduino controller would be housed. To ensure the stalk could remain in any position without succumbing to gravity, I incorporated a spring system that provided consistent resistance. Additionally, I implemented a 3:1 gear ratio between the potentiometer and the stalk, allowing for precise control with 0.16 inches of travel for each degree of stalk pivot.

To mount the system securely to the office chair, I used 80/20 extruded aluminum to extend from the chair's base and employed a custom SendCutSend bracket to hold everything in place. After assembling the entire unit, I mounted it to the chair, installed a software plug-in that enabled Arduino inputs to communicate with the flight simulator, and connected the Arduino to the computer.

The completed project functions exceptionally well, providing a much more immersive flight simulator experience by offering precise control and seamless integration.
